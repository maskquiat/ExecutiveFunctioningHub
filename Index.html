Memory Strategies

üóÇÔ∏è Categorization

What: Group related items together by category.

When to use: Memorizing vocabulary, historical facts, or any list of terms.

Example: Grouping U.S. Presidents by their political party or time period.

üìñ Narrative/Story Method

What: Create a story (even a bizarre one!) that links items together.

When to use: Retaining information in sequence or making connections.

Example: To remember Presidents, imagine George Washington living on Madison Street right at the corner of Jefferson Park where his dog plays with other canine friends (Monroe Street).

‚ù§Ô∏è Emotion Connection

What: Connect information to personal feelings or experiences.

When to use: Making content memorable and meaningful.

Example: Connect a water cycle lesson to a time you experienced flooding or drought.

üéµ Rhyme & Rhythm

What: Use music, rhythm, or rhymes to help remember information.

When to use: Memorizing formulas, spelling, or sequences.

Example: "In 1492, Columbus sailed the ocean blue"

üîó Personal Connection

What: Ask yourself "How do I connect to this?"

When to use: Making new material stick by relating it to what you already know.

Example: When learning about Hamlet, think about how you would react in that situation.

Note-Taking Strategies ‚úçÔ∏è

üìù Listen First, Then Write

During lectures, listen for key concepts BEFORE writing. Use bullet points and abbreviations. Go back afterward and fill in details while it's fresh in your mind.

Steps:

  1. Listen for key concepts before writing
  2. Record details first, going back and filling in main ideas later
  3. Use bullet points and abbreviations
  4. Ask clarifying questions
üìã Three-Column Method

Prepare your paper with three columns: Main Ideas | Supporting Details | Personal Connections

This helps you organize information AND make it meaningful to you.

üé® Multipurpose Notes

Active reading helps you:

  • Identify, highlight, and track important information for tests
  • Develop a "big picture" understanding of the material

Test-Taking Tips üìù

üìñ Before the Test
  • Follow teacher instructions carefully
  • Read directions and underline key words
  • Ask for clarification if needed
  • Look over the entire test before starting
  • Answer easy questions first
üìö For Reading Passages

Read the questions FIRST! This tells you what to look for.

Then look for context clues in the passage.

‚úçÔ∏è For Writing Tasks
  1. READ the prompt carefully
  2. THINK about what you know
  3. ORGANIZE your thoughts
  4. WRITE your response
üî¢ For Math Problems
  • Read problems carefully
  • Underline key information
  • Number the steps
  • Solve problem(s)
  • Check your work
  • Explain: How did I reach my answer? Why did I take those steps?
‚úÖ True/False Questions
  • Write T and F clearly
  • Read the entire statement carefully
  • Know that all parts must be true for the statement to be true
  • If there are exceptions, it is false
  • Look for cue words in FALSE: never, best, only, always, worst, first, not exactly, totally, none, all, because, every, invariably
  • Look for cue words in TRUE: generally, equally, frequently, less, seldom, mainly, often, usually, sometimes, probably, might, good
  • Watch for double negatives
  • If you don't know, guess (50% chance!)

SMART Goal Setting üéØ

What makes a goal SMART?

S - Specific

Clearly define what you want to achieve. Instead of "do better in math," try "improve my algebra test scores."

M - Measurable

How will you know when you've achieved it? "Raise my algebra grade from C to B."

A - Achievable

Is this realistic given your current situation and resources?

R - Relevant

Does this goal matter to you? Will it help you reach your bigger aspirations?

T - Time-bound

Set a deadline! "By the end of this semester."
